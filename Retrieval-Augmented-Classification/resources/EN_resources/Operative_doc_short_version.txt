Level 1 (Basic Level): Visualization
The student bases their thinking on basic geometric concepts, such as simple shapes, primarily through visual observation of a shape as a whole, without explicitly referring to the properties of its components.
They understand that there are differences and similarities between shapes.
They understand the preservation of shape of figures in different positions.
The student does not perceive properties as characterizing a concept.
The student:
Verbal
1.01. Describes shapes according to their general appearance (e.g., a rectangle "looks like a window", a parallelogram "looks like a tilted rectangle", an angle "looks like clock hands").
1.04. Questions in which there is no answer or no mathematical justification will be classified as Level 1.
Applied
1.08. Drags the shape in the applet and uses the shape's name to justify its existence without mentioning additional properties.

Level 2: Analysis
The student understands that geometric concepts have properties, and that these properties can be used to distinguish between concepts. The necessary properties of a concept are established.
The student does not see how properties relate to one another; does not formulate or use formal definitions; does not explain inclusion relations (subset); does not see the need for logical explanations of empirically discovered generalizations.
The student:
Verbal
2.01. Recalls and uses appropriate vocabulary for components and relations (e.g., opposite sides, corresponding congruent angles, diagonals bisecting each other).
2.02. Describes a class of shapes (e.g., parallelogram) in terms of its properties, but does not link or explain relationships between shape families. For example, does not conclude that every square is a rectangle. Understands that geometric concepts have properties, and that these properties can be used to distinguish between concepts.
2.06. Lists a set of properties for a shape or solid, for example an isosceles triangle has equal base angles, the altitude bisects the base, the apex angle bisector coincides with the median and altitude.
2.07. Identifies a shape by its properties rather than by appearance alone ("it looks like a square" — this is not Level 2, it is Level 1). Can say: "Because it has four equal sides and right angles it is a square" — this is Level 2. The student does not necessarily conclude that this is the definition of a square; they may list various properties of the shape without seeing them as necessary and sufficient conditions.
Applied
2.08. Identifies and examines relationships between components of a shape (e.g., equality of opposite sides in a parallelogram).
2.11. Compares shapes by their properties (e.g., notes how a square and a rectangle are similar and different in terms of sides and angles, including number of sides, side lengths, angle types, etc.).
2.14. When identifying shapes, explaining their identification, and deciding about unrecognised shapes, uses a list of necessary properties rather than determining sufficient conditions.


Level 3: Abstraction
The student is able to operate with relations between concepts or shapes (logical/hierarchical connections between geometric concepts, membership, inclusion, necessity, sufficiency). They logically order the properties of concepts (what follows from what); accept a logical partial order among shape types, including inclusion relations; use and develop abstract definitions (can use an existing definition or formulate one independently); and are able to distinguish between the necessity and sufficiency of a set of properties for determining a concept.
The student does not grasp the meaning of proof in the axiomatic sense, and cannot yet create connections between networks of theorems (i.e., which theorem follows from another, etc.).
The student:
Verbal
3.02. Formulates statements demonstrating relationships, generalizations, and inclusion relations between shapes.
3.03. Uses logical language: "if... then", "all", "some", "each", "none", "at least" (G) "provided that", "since", "because", "therefore", "every ___ is also ___".
Applied
3.04. States a necessary and sufficient property of a shape (e.g., "a parallelogram because the diagonals bisect each other" — this is Level 3. Another example: a parallelogram whose diagonals are equal is a rectangle). Identifies or provides a minimal set of properties that can characterise a concept, shape, or solid.
3.06. Orders and connects properties; can deduce one property from another.
3.07. Identifies shapes belonging to more than one class; uses properties to determine whether one class of shapes is included in another. Can classify a specific shape into two different groups. For example, a square belongs to both the class of rectangles and the class of rhombuses.
3.09. Definitions: applies definitions, modifies definitions (e.g., provides an equivalent definition for the same concept), formulates definitions, immediately accepts and applies definitions of new concepts, recognises equivalence of definitions.
3.10. Presents informal justifications (using diagrams, cut-out shapes, or other materials); discovers new properties through simple inference (often based, at least in part, on empirical evidence).
3.16. Based on general statements learned or known, can infer facts.
3.18. Inclusion relations between classes of shapes. For example, the student explains that every square is a rectangle (because it has all the properties of a rectangle), but not every rectangle is a square (because not every rectangle has four equal sides). Does not stop at listing properties (analysis level), and does not merely sort by appearance (visualisation level).
3.19. If the student says they drag or imagine and tracks the properties of the shape (not only the shape's name but additional properties), or generates many different examples — this indicates generalisation from examples, Level 3. Seeing or identifying from one or two examples, or saying "I created it and saw that it is correct", is Level 2.
3.20. Comparison of two shapes in an applet: the student refers to properties that are preserved throughout the entire drag (generalisation), and mentions more than one property (in the applet).
3.21. Comparison of two shapes (not in an applet): the student refers to inclusion relations and shape families, rather than listing a set of shared properties as in Level 2.
3.22. Given a new (non-Euclidean) geometric system, the student translates its concepts into Euclidean geometry concepts but does not answer the question fully.


Level 4: Deductive Reasoning
The student thinks formally within a mathematical system, including undefined terms, axioms, a basic logical system, definitions, and theorems.
They recognize the need for and structure of undefined terms, definitions, axioms, and theorems.
They implicitly accept the axioms of Euclidean geometry.
They rely on proof as the ultimate authority in deciding on the truth of a mathematical claim.
Required: writes a complete proof based on what is given, or states claims together with their justifications.
The student:
Applied
4.06. Deduces properties of objects from given or known information; this includes proving relationships that were explained informally at Level 2.
4.08. Uses rules of logic to develop a proof. Starts from definitions, proceeds through axioms and theorems, and draws conclusions rigorously. Does not solve problems by intuition or a single example — always constructs a general proof. For instance, proves that all diagonals in a rectangle are equal using definitions and axioms, not just based on a drawing or one example. Not merely an intuitive explanation or general discussion of properties (abstraction or analysis level).

Level 5: Rigor
Verbal
5.01. Explicitly refers to definitions from different axiomatic systems and compares them according to different mathematical goals or according to the structure of the system.
5.02. Describes how a geometric property, concept, or proof changes or remains valid across different axiomatic systems (e.g., comparing Euclidean and hyperbolic geometry).
Applied
5.12. Understands that the identification of a given mathematical object or concept is not an absolute act but depends on the geometric context in which one works; that is, the properties characterising the object may change from one context to another.
